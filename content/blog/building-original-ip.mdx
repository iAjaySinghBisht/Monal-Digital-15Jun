Every original IP we have ever shipped started life as something embarrassingly
small: a doodle in the corner of a notebook, a voice someone did in a meeting, a
single line of dialogue that made the room laugh.

## From sketch to world

The trap most studios fall into is designing the *show* before they understand
the *character*. We do it the other way around. Before we write a single
episode, we ask three questions:

- **What does this character want, and what's in the way?**
- **What does the world look like through their eyes?**
- **Why would a seven-year-old draw this character at the back of class?**

That last one matters more than any pitch deck. If kids won't redraw it, it
won't travel.

## The first ten seconds

A pilot lives or dies in its opening. We prototype the first ten seconds of
motion before we lock any design, because motion reveals personality faster than
any turnaround sheet. A walk cycle tells you whether a character is brave,
nervous, or up to no good.

> Design tells you who a character is. Animation tells you who they *become*.

## Where it goes next

Once the character holds up, the world builds itself: supporting cast, recurring
gags, a visual grammar that can stretch across a hundred episodes without
getting tired. That's the moment a sketch becomes an IP.
